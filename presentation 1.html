Problem

The objective

Pick at most 1,000 customers to contact. Every contact is a bet: earn €10 if they convert, lose €5 if they don't. Each original feature column costs €200, paid once regardless of how many models you train on it.

Score = 10 × TP  −  5 × FP  −  200 × k

k = number of original columns purchased. Derived features (e.g. x₁ + x₂) count as k = 2. The 1,000-contact cap means false negatives are invisible to the metric.

What the formula forces you to do:

  • Rank all 5,000 customers, cut at 1,000
  • Feature engineering is structurally penalized
  • Ensembling is free — same k, multiple models

Feature selection

Finding the right k

Forward greedy search: add one feature at a time, keep what improves the OOF €-score, stop when the next best candidate can't cover its €200 cost.

Why the cheap screen misleads

1. XGBoost screen (k=6): all 21 candidates score below the k=5 baseline. Conclusion: "stop at k=5."
2. XGBoost at k=6 lacks the model capacity to benefit from the extra column. The screen is asking the wrong question.
3. Full pipeline (TabPFN + TabICL + CatBoost + RF): V380 at k=6 wins by +€819 over the k=5 supervised baseline.
4. Rule: use a cheap proxy to shortlist candidates, never to bury them.

Semi-supervised

Pseudo-labeling the test set

We have 5,000 unlabeled test rows. A supervised CatBoost+RF pass scores them independently — no foundation-model output involved, no circular dependency. Top 2,500 by rank become pseudo-positives, bottom 2,500 become pseudo-negatives.

1. 10-fold CatBoost+RF on labeled train → unbiased test scores
2. Top/bottom 2,500 by rank → pseudo-labels frozen
3. All four ensemble members now train on 10,000 rows (5k labeled + 5k pseudo)
4. Re-run steps 1–2 on updated beliefs ("v2 pseudo") — 12.2% of labels flip
5. Check again ("v3") — 0.2% (12 rows) flip → converged, stop
The supervised pass for pseudo-labels must not use TabPFN or TabICL. Including them would mean the foundation models train against their own slightly-wrong opinions.

Takeaways

What this project actually taught us

Start with foundation models
TabPFN and TabICL took us from €5,520 to €5,630 without a single hyperparameter search. On a small, anonymized dataset, a model pre-trained on millions of synthetic tables starts from a better prior than anything you can grid-search by hand. Start here, not at the end.
Cheap screens shortlist — they don't kill
Twice in this project a fast screen told us to stop: once on k=6, once on the 6th-feature choice. Both times the full pipeline reversed the call. Use a proxy to generate candidates. Never use it to bury them.
Pseudo-labeling works if it's honest
Worth +€510 OOF — but only because the labeling step used a completely separate supervised pipeline. Let foundation-model output feed back into its own training labels and you're training on slightly-wrong self-opinions.
The cost rule gave us ensembling for free
Counting €200 per original column makes feature engineering expensive and ensembling free. Four models on six columns costs exactly what one model would. That's the structural reason a four-way blend made sense here.
Person C. ~35 sec. "Four things we'd carry forward. First: foundation models early, not as a last resort. Second: cheap screens generate candidates, they don't eliminate them — verifying a 'this won't work' is expensive, but missing your best result is worse. Third: pseudo-labeling was our biggest gain, but only because the labeling step was kept completely independent of the evaluated models.
